Indian History Chronology

30 defining events across three eras — Ancient, Medieval, and Modern — tracing India's journey across 5,000 years of history.

Ancient India  ·  c.2500 BCE – 700 CE Medieval India  ·  700 CE – 1857 Modern India  ·  1857 – 1950
Ancient

Ancient India

c. 2500 BCE – 700 CE

c. 2500 – 1900 BCE

Peak of the Indus Valley Civilisation

The Harappan civilisation reached its height with planned cities at Mohenjo-daro and Harappa — featuring grid streets, covered drainage, and standardised weights. It was among the largest and most advanced urban societies of the ancient world, home to an estimated one million people across hundreds of settlements.

Harappan

c. 1500 BCE

Composition of the Rigveda

The earliest of the four Vedas was composed during the early Vedic period — representing the oldest surviving Sanskrit literature. Its 1,028 hymns form the foundational text of the Hindu tradition and shaped Indian philosophy, ritual, and society for the next three thousand years.

Vedic Age

c. 563 – 483 BCE

Life of Siddhartha Gautama — the Buddha

Born a prince in Lumbini, Siddhartha attained enlightenment at Bodh Gaya and became the Buddha. His teachings on the Four Noble Truths and the Eightfold Path founded Buddhism — a religion that spread from India across all of Asia and eventually the world, profoundly transforming civilisation.

Buddhism

321 BCE

Chandragupta Maurya Founds the Maurya Empire

Guided by strategist Chanakya, Chandragupta Maurya overthrew the Nanda dynasty and established the first pan-Indian empire — stretching from Persia to Bengal. His statecraft treatise, the Arthashastra, remains one of the most sophisticated works of political economy in the ancient world.

Maurya Empire

261 BCE

Battle of Kalinga — Ashoka Embraces Dhamma

Emperor Ashoka's brutal conquest of Kalinga killed over 100,000 people and deeply shook him. He converted to Buddhism, renounced warfare, and issued rock and pillar edicts across the subcontinent promoting non-violence, tolerance, and welfare. His edicts are India's earliest surviving inscriptions and a landmark in the history of human governance.

Ashoka

c. 320 – 550 CE

The Gupta Empire — India's Golden Age

The Gupta period saw extraordinary achievements in science, mathematics, literature, and art. Aryabhatta calculated pi and proposed a heliocentric model. Kalidasa wrote celebrated Sanskrit dramas. The decimal number system and the concept of zero — invented in India during this era — went on to transform world mathematics and science.

Golden Age

c. 300 – 600 CE

Rise of the Pallava and Chalukya Dynasties in South India

While northern empires rose and fell, South India saw the Pallavas build the magnificent Shore Temple at Mahabalipuram and the Chalukyas construct the stunning rock-cut cave temples at Badami and Aihole. These dynasties established the distinct Dravidian architectural tradition and expanded Indian cultural influence into Southeast Asia.

South India

c. 2nd Century BCE – 6th Century CE

Ajanta Cave Paintings

Over seven centuries, Buddhist monks carved and painted 30 magnificent caves into the rocky gorge at Ajanta in Maharashtra. The murals — depicting the Jataka tales with extraordinary naturalistic detail — represent the pinnacle of ancient Indian painting and remain among the finest artworks in human history.

Buddhist Art

606 – 647 CE

Reign of Emperor Harsha of Kannauj

Harsha briefly reunited much of northern India under a just and cultured administration. A patron of Buddhism and Sanskrit literature, he himself wrote plays. The Chinese traveller Xuanzang visited during his reign and left detailed records of Indian society, religion, and daily life — invaluable to historians today.

Harsha

c. 850 – 1279 CE

The Chola Empire at Its Height

The imperial Cholas built one of India's greatest maritime empires, with their navy dominating the Bay of Bengal and launching campaigns as far as Malaysia and Sumatra. Their architectural legacy — the Brihadeeswarar Temple at Thanjavur, built by Raja Raja Chola I — stands as one of the supreme achievements of Indian temple architecture.

Chola Empire
Medieval

Medieval India

700 CE – 1857

712 CE

Arab Conquest of Sindh

Muhammad bin Qasim led an Umayyad Arab army into Sindh, defeating Raja Dahir and establishing the first permanent Islamic rule in the Indian subcontinent. This opened the door to centuries of Islamic cultural, architectural, and philosophical exchange with India, reshaping the subcontinent's religious and demographic landscape.

Islamic Entry

1000 – 1027 CE

Mahmud of Ghazni's Raids into India

The Ghaznavid sultan Mahmud of Ghazni made 17 raids into the Indian subcontinent, looting vast wealth including the famous sack of the Somnath temple in Gujarat (1025). His campaigns weakened the Rajput kingdoms and opened northwestern India to further Central Asian incursions over the following centuries.

Ghaznavid

1192 CE

Second Battle of Tarain — Prithviraj Chauhan Defeated

Muhammad of Ghor defeated Prithviraj Chauhan III — the last major Hindu emperor of northern India — at the Second Battle of Tarain near Delhi. This decisive defeat ended Rajput supremacy in the north and opened the way for the establishment of the Delhi Sultanate, transforming the political and cultural character of the subcontinent.

Delhi Sultanate

1206 CE

Establishment of the Delhi Sultanate

Qutb ud-Din Aibak founded the Delhi Sultanate — the first major Islamic empire in India — after the death of Muhammad of Ghor. Over the next 320 years, five successive dynasties (the Mamluks, Khaljis, Tughlaqs, Sayyids, and Lodis) ruled from Delhi, leaving a permanent imprint on Indian architecture, culture, administration, and language.

Delhi Sultanate

1336 CE

Founding of the Vijayanagara Empire

Harihara I and Bukka Raya I founded Vijayanagara — one of the greatest Hindu kingdoms of the medieval world. At its peak under Krishnadevaraya (r. 1509–29), Vijayanagara was one of the most prosperous cities on earth, a bastion of Hindu culture, art, and learning whose ruins at Hampi remain a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Vijayanagara

1498 CE

Vasco da Gama Reaches India

Portuguese explorer Vasco da Gama arrived at Calicut (Kozhikode) on the Malabar Coast, opening a direct sea route between Europe and India. This transformed global trade, broke the Arab monopoly on the spice trade, and marked the beginning of sustained European engagement with India — setting in motion events that would ultimately lead to colonialism.

European Contact

1526 CE

First Battle of Panipat — Babur Founds the Mughal Empire

Babur, a descendant of Timur and Genghis Khan, defeated the last Lodi sultan Ibrahim Lodi at Panipat and established the Mughal Empire — one of the greatest empires in world history. At its zenith under Aurangzeb, the Mughal Empire covered nearly the entire Indian subcontinent and was one of the wealthiest states on earth.

Mughal Empire

1556 CE

Akbar's Reign Begins — Era of Religious Tolerance

Emperor Akbar ascended the Mughal throne at age 13 and became one of the greatest rulers in Indian history. He abolished the jizya tax on non-Muslims, married Hindu Rajput princesses, invited scholars of all faiths to his court, and created Din-i-Ilahi — a syncretic spiritual movement. His reign unified the subcontinent through administration, culture, and enlightened policy.

Mughal

1631 – 1648 CE

Construction of the Taj Mahal

Emperor Shah Jahan commissioned the Taj Mahal at Agra in memory of his wife Mumtaz Mahal. Over 20,000 artisans worked for 17 years to build this ivory-white marble marvel — the supreme expression of Mughal architecture. It is today the most visited monument in India and a UNESCO World Heritage Site recognised globally as a symbol of eternal love.

Mughal Architecture

1757 CE

Battle of Plassey — British Begin Control of India

The British East India Company under Robert Clive defeated the Nawab of Bengal, Siraj ud-Daulah, at the Battle of Plassey. Victory here gave the Company control of Bengal — the richest province in India — and began a process of territorial and economic expansion that would eventually result in British dominion over the entire subcontinent.

British Era
Modern

Modern India

1857 – 1950

1857

The First War of Indian Independence

A widespread uprising — also called the Sepoy Mutiny — broke out against the British East India Company across northern and central India. Though suppressed, it marked India's first organised armed resistance to colonial rule. In its aftermath, the British Crown dissolved the East India Company and assumed direct governance of India, beginning the British Raj.

Independence Movement

1885

Founding of the Indian National Congress

The Indian National Congress (INC) was founded in Bombay, initially as a platform for educated Indians to petition the British government. Under successive leaders it grew into the primary vehicle for the Indian independence movement, eventually led by figures including Bal Gangadhar Tilak, Gopal Krishna Gokhale, and most famously, Mahatma Gandhi.

Nationalism

1905

Partition of Bengal and the Swadeshi Movement

Viceroy Curzon's partition of Bengal along religious lines sparked massive public outrage and gave birth to the Swadeshi movement — a campaign to boycott British goods and promote Indian-made products. It marked a turning point in Indian nationalism, transforming the independence movement from a polite petition into a mass political force.

Swadeshi

1915

Mahatma Gandhi Returns to India

Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi returned to India from South Africa and transformed the independence movement. His philosophy of Satyagraha (non-violent resistance) and his ability to mobilise millions across caste, class, and religion made him the central figure of the freedom struggle and one of the most influential leaders in world history.

Gandhi

1919

Jallianwala Bagh Massacre

British General Reginald Dyer ordered his troops to open fire on a peaceful crowd gathered at Jallianwala Bagh in Amritsar, killing at least 379 people (with estimates running far higher) and wounding over 1,200. The massacre turned moderate opinion across India decisively against British rule and is one of the darkest events of the colonial period.

Colonial Atrocity

1930

Gandhi's Salt March — Dandi March

Gandhi led a 240-mile march from Sabarmati Ashram to the coastal village of Dandi to produce salt in defiance of the British salt tax. The march captured global attention and triggered the Civil Disobedience Movement, drawing millions into direct action against colonial rule. It remains one of the most iconic acts of non-violent protest in history.

Civil Disobedience

1942

Quit India Movement

Gandhi launched the Quit India Movement at the Bombay session of the Congress, demanding an immediate end to British rule. His call — "Do or Die" — led to the arrest of virtually the entire Congress leadership and widespread civil unrest. The movement demonstrated to Britain that governing India was no longer sustainable and accelerated the path to independence.

Quit India

15 August 1947

Indian Independence — End of British Rule

At midnight on 15 August 1947, India became an independent nation after nearly 200 years of British colonial rule. Jawaharlal Nehru delivered his famous "Tryst with Destiny" speech at the Red Fort in Delhi. Independence also brought Partition — the creation of Pakistan — leading to one of the largest mass migrations in human history and immense communal violence.

Independence

30 January 1948

Assassination of Mahatma Gandhi

Mahatma Gandhi was assassinated by Nathuram Godse at Birla House in New Delhi. Gandhi's death shocked the world and plunged India into mourning. He remains the Father of the Nation and his philosophy of non-violent resistance influenced liberation movements globally — from the American civil rights movement to anti-apartheid struggles in South Africa.

Gandhi

26 January 1950

India Becomes a Republic — Constitution Adopted

India's Constitution — the longest written constitution of any nation — came into force on 26 January 1950, making India a sovereign democratic republic. Drafted primarily by Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, it guaranteed fundamental rights to all citizens regardless of caste, religion, or gender. Republic Day is celebrated on this date every year as India's founding as a constitutional democracy.

Republic
